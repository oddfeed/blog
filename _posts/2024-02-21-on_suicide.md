# On Suicide
In the UK, suicide only became associated with mental illness to allow widows to mount a legal defence, a husband would kill himself (which was a criminal act) then because he was now considered a criminal his assets would be seized by the government, eventually the argument was made that the deceased should not be held criminally responsible because if a person intentionally takes their own life they must be insane. This allowed the widowers or children of the deceased to claim the assets without government seizure.

The consequences of this I believe are grave, suicide is a problem of philosophy, killing yourself however can be a problem of illness - to clarify how I intend to use these terms I don&rsquo;t believe suicidal people, or those that commit suicide are mentally ill, theirs is a problem of spirit - Those who kill themselves or wish to kill themselves however are almost always mentally ill.

Those that are mentally ill in a form that alters their capacity for assessing their current reality or future with accuracy cannot commit suicide they can only ever kill themselves, This is a distinction I think should be emphasised strongly, sucidality and suicide come about when an individual wants to take action to end their life not because of a chemical imbalance, flawed assessment or delusions about themselves or the world but from their correct assessment of their life, the world and their future; it&rsquo;s not a choice made in haste but an inevitability steadily marched toward.

# The failure to address suicide.

This is where I believe the failing starts, if someone who talks of suicide is seen as someone who in some capacity is detached from reality the underlying problem will never be addressed, the individual will be seen as incorrect in whatever conclusion they&rsquo;ve reached, but the individual is in most cases capable of knowing their reasoning is sound so if the doctor or whomever refuses to engage with an open mind your concerns are dismissed as invalid - this does nothing to solve the problem of suicide, neither will medication, because the issue isn&rsquo;t one of illness so how could medication possibly hope to resolve it - I personally hold that a similar effect takes place with depression which is why medication doesn&rsquo;t offer much to the &rsquo;depressed&rsquo; who&rsquo;re suffering through problems of philosophy as apposed to the depressed that suffer from issues of chemicals the fact this distinction is not tested prior to treatment will continue to let down a lot of those who seek treatment or more aptly, help.

I see no way for the problem of suicidality to be &rsquo;solved&rsquo;, nor suicidal thoughts for that matter, for by my definitions if an individual is suicidal then they&rsquo;re reached the logical conclusion so unless whatever complaints they have are resolved the underlying suicidal thoughts cannot be and the complaints that lead an individual to be suicidal are not ones that will likely be resolved, The only reasonable approach I can see to the problem is confrontation not just with the complaints as a whole but with all their parts and even down to the interactions between parts, once this point of examination has been completed the thoughts of suicide become less of an intention and more of a compulsion, this may seem counter intuitive but a compulsion can be fought against whereas an intention is a desire, compulsion can and often does exist without a desire for the thing itself it exists usually with a desire for the secondary parts of the thing- such as being dead or no longer being alive as opposed to committing suicide itself.

Ultimately a pursuit must take place of something that can never be caught; An endless, furious and passionate chase that requires you to not just be alive, but to be engaged in life, to take part in - separating from the world will just bring the desire flooding back so things that require interaction with both the natural and industrialised world and with people of them must be pursued, but the individual will still be suicidal and in all likely hood will still commit suicide, the ability to express in excruciating detail why you&rsquo;re suicidal and the things that keep you attached to the world show a deeper level of understanding, one that is capable (paradoxically) of delaying the inevitable for as long as you wish.

# The validity of suicide.

Stigma and selfish logic surrounds suicide and likely always will but the act of suicide is not an immoral one and believing it is impedes your ability to confront it because you believe theres something wrong with the feeling, the desire or if you&rsquo;ve reached that stage the compulsion but without the confrontation you will suffer much more than with it. The three main reasons given against suicide don&rsquo;t hold up and are likely just post hoc rationalisations since it seems unlikely that if a person thought about what they&rsquo;ve said they wouldn&rsquo;t realise it doesn&rsquo;t hold up.

The main reason given is suicide is never the answer, the people who say this likely have no problem with euthanasia in cases of severe illness - however they draw a distinction that the persons life is all downhill, this is horrible and reprehensible reasoning that would give a thumbs up to a treatment resistant depressed person (someone suffering from a legitimate mental illness) being euthanized because their prognosis is likely similar, there is nothing the doctors can do and all that is left is suffering, the justifications for euthanasia will apply either to mentally ill people in the world right now or to hypothetical ones in the future if we could (a) confirm that someones depression is caused by dysfunction in the brain (b) confirm any treatments we know of will not be effective, including things like exercise, how could you justify telling them suicide is not the answer without hypocrisy? the justification of &ldquo;it&rsquo;s never the answer&rdquo; only avoids hypocrisy if the person also doesn&rsquo;t believe in euthanasia though this belief shows a deep level of immorality when it comes to controlling the lives of others.

The second common reason is in my opinion the most evil, &ldquo;it&rsquo;s selfish to kill yourself&rdquo; this is nothing more than a manipulation, most people are conditioned to this belief but if you meet someone who firmly holds it not through conditioning but through their own internal reasoning then be very wary of this person - the logic when broken down into parts and devoid (as much as I&rsquo;m capable) of loaded language becomes

an individual whos suffering is so great they wish to commit suicide should continue to endure that suffering indefinitely because I (the other) do not wish to mourn them.

Hold onto that for a minute and take in the logic used there and also the extension that the &rsquo;other&rsquo; believes the individual to be the selfish one, the &rsquo;other&rsquo; or anyone who tries to restrict an individuals suicide, through physical restriction or emotional manipulation like above is definition-ally removing their autonomy to a very large degree.

The third reason I refer to as &rsquo;obligation to live&rsquo; this doesn&rsquo;t however take one form but follows a general consistency of you have been given a life so it is an affront to that life and those that gave it to you to end it, I do not believe this to be the case, if the life was given to me to be mine then I can do with it as I wish without it being an affront, surely the reason I was given life was to do with it as I wish - this issue however seems more common in parents than in any other group I&rsquo;ve spoken to about suicide, this includes friends, family, doctors and strangers, it seems to stem from not seeing the child as a person but as an extension of the parents property, much like a dog. A good thought experiment to see this is the willingness to remove free will from a child but not a stranger. If a homeless man who&rsquo;s never taken heroin decides he will being using it and you&rsquo;re presented a button that removes a part of his free will so he can never make the choice to do heroin would you press it? what about if the homeless man is swapped out with your child (hypothetical or otherwise) you&rsquo;re much more likely to hit the button and rationalise it as &ldquo;I just want to keep my child safe&rdquo; &ldquo;I just want whats best for them&rdquo; - but to me the position that you get to decide whats best for them and then remove their free will and autonomy based upon that shows clearly and indisputably you don&rsquo;t respect them or view their life as theirs, I think this is re-enforced by the idea you gave them their life, in a physical sense this is in part true but what you mean more generally is you formed what they are through upbringing - but every loving parent would attest that raising their child made them who they are or even gave meaning to their life but imagine how disrespected the parent would feel if the child said that they gave their parent their life, either both the parent and child gave each other life or neither did, the argument of giving physical life also doesn&rsquo;t hold up with the brief consideration theres no life before birth/conception (or wherever you draw the line) one would be completely indifferent to not being born and one didn&rsquo;t ask for it, there is obligation to those that raised you for the raising of you, but a life you didn&rsquo;t ask for, that was created by your parents to bring them happiness creates no obligation on your part.

When restricting (or trying to restrict) someones ability to commit suicide through methods such as physical restriction or emotional manipulation there needs to be an incredible degree of certainty that you have a right to attempt to or to actually restrict someones ability to make that choice because it&rsquo;s nothing short of a restriction to someones autonomy, of their ownership of their own life I think the only compelling reason is that the individuals ability to make that choice is impaired, a few common reasons is drugs, mental illness or otherwise dysfunctional thought processes. To remove someones freedom, freewill and ownership of their life requires you to make an incredibly compelling case that the individuals capacity to make this choice is impaired in some way. Anything else will always lead not just to indefensible acts but to hypocrisy.

# How should it end?

Since I&rsquo;m a single person, with a single set of experiences it seems wholly irresponsible for me to make any kind of prescriptive claims about the taking of ones own life. What seems to be clearly demonstrated however is that most deaths are drawn out much longer than they should be, the end of ones life is nothing above suffering due not to an attachment to life but because of a fear of death, do not be afraid to pick the option of death, be afraid of being unwilling to make the choice itself - this is where true horror resides, not in the person who has let go of life but in the person who clutches onto it for fear of death.

Suicide is ultimately a choice of the individual however considerations of obligation to others should be made, earlier I explained that being obligated to be alive is a disgusting justification for why someone should not commit suicide - but an obligation of helping those you care about avoid suffering is worth serious exploration and consideration, You can be both deeply suicidal and happy, but a parent who has lost a child might permanently lose their capacity to be happy due to the triggering of mental illness, there seems to be a rush on suicide that I both understand and detest. I&rsquo;m brought back to the old quote &ldquo;relax, you have your entire life to kill yourself&rdquo; or in our case commit suicide, The assessment as to how long you&rsquo;re obligated to put off your suicide for others is worth more serious thought than perhaps anything else, once the time has been reached in which you no longer feel that obligation you have achieved true freedom of consequences (in terms of yourself) you can do any form of self destructive behaviour you wish if you reach a point of no return then simply commit suicide.

This freedom itself might give you reason to delay your suicide indefinitely until you do reach the point of no return but before suicide think about if you are doing anything wrong by ending your life, have you done your absolute best to alleviate the suffering others will endure upon your suicide, to help them understand not only why the act was committed but explained how it was unavoidable.

If all criteria is met it should end however the person chooses, having choice over ones own death is the ultimate act of freedom - grasping onto life no matter how great your suffering due to fear is however akin to not being alive in the first place.

There are no suicidal people who chose not to commit suicide, just those who die before they do.
